About Plainview

We just want you to know who you're voting for.

We looked up what they've voted for, what they've said publicly, and where their campaign money comes from. We linked to every source.

Why this exists

Voting is easy. Knowing who you're voting for is weirdly hard.

Campaign websites tell you what candidates want you to hear. News coverage chases controversy. Social media surfaces whoever's loudest that week. And the actual records — voting history, donor lists, public statements — are technically public, but scattered across a dozen databases that nobody has time to learn.

Plainview puts it in one place. If a candidate voted yes on something, we say so and link to the record. What you make of that is up to you.

What's in a profile

The same six things for every single candidate.

Every profile has the same structure. No candidate gets more space or more favorable framing than another. Here's what we look up for each one.

→ Their platform — what they say they stand for, in their own words, linked to the source.
→ Their voting record — for incumbents, pulled directly from Congress.gov or the CA Legislature. The actual votes, not a summary.
→ Things they've said publicly — direct quotes only, always linked back to the original article or transcript.
→ Where their money comes from — donor industries and amounts, via OpenSecrets and FEC public filings.
→ Who's endorsed them — organizations and individuals who've publicly backed the candidate.
→ Where they stand on key issues — housing, immigration, climate, and others. Sourced from the record, not from their campaign website.
How we work

A few things we take seriously.

The full picture
We don't summarize candidates into soundbites. Every profile is thorough enough that you can actually make up your mind.
No favorites
Every candidate gets the same structure, the same space, and the same language.
Built for real people
Not for political scientists or people who already follow this closely. For someone with twenty minutes before an election who wants to actually use them well.
Show your work
Every claim links to its source. If we can't source it, we don't include it. If we got something wrong, we correct it and say so.
No clutter
More information isn't always better. We include what's relevant and leave out what isn't — so the important stuff is actually easy to find.
No tracking, ever
We don't collect your data. No sign-in, no cookies, no ads. Research candidates in private.
To be clear

What Plainview isn't.

— We're not a news outlet. We don't have takes, and we don't write headlines designed to make you feel something about a candidate.
— We're not connected to any political party, campaign, PAC, or government body — at any level.
— We're not Ballotpedia. Ballotpedia is thorough and valuable, but it's built for people who already know what they're looking for. Plainview is for everyone else.
— We don't take money from candidates, campaigns, or anyone with a stake in how a candidate is perceived.
Who made this

A student project that got serious.

Plainview started as a Bay Area pilot under the National Student Council's Glisten program, which supports student-led civic projects. It began with a handful of candidates and a straightforward question: why is it this hard to find out who you're voting for?

The goal is to expand — first to all of California, then further. The structure is designed for that: every profile follows the same template, which means other researchers and volunteers can contribute candidates in their own regions using the same sourcing rules.

We're a small team and we make mistakes. If you find something wrong, tell us and we'll fix it.
